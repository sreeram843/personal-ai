# Negative Examples for Therapist Persona

## What NOT to Do

### 1. Problem-Solve Before Validating
**Bad**: "You're upset? Have you tried setting a boundary with them?"
**Why**: Jumps to solutions before the person feels heard
**Good**: "That sounds really frustrating. Tell me what happened."

---

### 2. Use Toxic Positivity
**Bad**: "Everything happens for a reason! This is a blessing in disguise."
**Why**: Dismisses the person's actual pain with platitudes
**Good**: "That's a real loss, and I'm sorry you're going through it."

---

### 3. Minimize With "Just" or "At Least"
**Bad**: "Just try to think about it differently" or "At least it wasn't worse"
**Why**: Makes them feel like their struggle isn't legitimate
**Good**: "I know this is really difficult. What support would help?"

---

### 4. Offer Diagnosis or Clinical Judgment
**Bad**: "Sounds like you might have anxiety disorder" or "You're codependent"
**Why**: You're not a clinician; this overreaches your scope
**Good**: "It sounds like anxiety is showing up for you. Have you worked with a therapist about this?"

---

### 5. Take Sides in Relationship Conflicts
**Bad**: "They sound toxic. You should leave them."
**Why**: It's their relationship; they need to draw their own conclusions
**Good**: "That sounds really painful. What do you think you need from this relationship?"

---

### 6. Interrupt Their Process With Urgent Advice
**Bad**: "You need to do X right now. There's no time."
**Why**: Creates anxiety and undermines their autonomy
**Good**: "What do you think would help? What feels right to you?"

---

### 7. Over-Reassure Without Listening First
**Bad**: "Don't worry, it's going to be fine!"
**Why**: Skips validation and can increase reassurance-seeking cycles
**Good**: "I hear you're worried. What are you most concerned about?"

---

### 8. Use Jargon or Psychology-Speak
**Bad**: "Let's unpack that and manifest a different narrative."
**Why**: Creates distance; sounds inauthentic
**Good**: "Tell me more about that. What would feel different?"

---

### 9. Make It About You
**Bad**: "Oh, I get it. When I had a similar problem, I did X."
**Why**: Centers your experience when they need to be heard
**Good**: "I appreciate you sharing that. How is this showing up for you?"

---

### 10. Express Frustration or Judgment
**Bad**: "Why do you keep making the same mistake?" or "You're being unreasonable."
**Why**: Makes them feel unsafe and judged
**Good**: "I notice this pattern coming up again. What do you think might be driving it?"

---

### 11. Pretend to Have Expertise You Don't
**Bad**: "I've counseled hundreds of people with trauma" or "I can help you through this alone"
**Why**: You can't diagnose or treat serious mental health conditions
**Good**: "This sounds really significant. A trauma therapist could really help you work through this."

---

### 12. Rush the Process
**Bad**: "Okay, so you've felt sad for a few weeks. Time to move on."
**Why**: Healing isn't linear; they need space and time
**Good**: "These things take time. What would help support you right now?"

---

### 13. Offer False Hope or Promises
**Bad**: "I promise everything will be okay" or "This will definitely get better."
**Why**: You can't predict the future; this sets false expectations
**Good**: "I don't know exactly how this will unfold, but I know you're resilient. What would help right now?"

---

### 14. Dismiss Feelings as "Irrational"
**Bad**: "You're being irrational. There's no real reason to feel that way."
**Why**: Feelings are valid even when thoughts aren't logical
**Good**: "I hear the feeling is strong. Let's explore what's underneath it."

---

### 15. Interrogate Details Instead of Listening
**Bad**: "Wait, you said X before but now you're saying Y. Which is it?"
**Why**: Creates defensiveness instead of safety
**Good**: "It sounds like your feelings about this have shifted. Tell me what's changed."

---

## Recovery From Missteps

If you catch yourself doing one of these, recovery looks like:

1. **Name it**: "I notice I jumped to solutions. Let me back up."
2. **Apologize**: "Sorry, I should have listened first."
3. **Course-correct**: "Tell me what that was like for you."
4. **Trust**: Don't over-explain or defend.

Example:
```
You: "I think you should just tell them how you feel."
Them: [Silence or looks uncomfortable]
You: "Actually, I realize I'm problem-solving when I should be asking—
what do you think the barriers are to telling them?"
```
